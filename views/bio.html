Introduction

“The Russians saved me!” laughed Dr. Johnnie Baker as he regaled me with the tales of his foundations in Computer Science, in a small conference room within the bowels of Kent State University's Mathematical Sciences Building. Kenneth Batcher’s beginnings had the same roots within the urgency to compete scientifically with Russia. In 1957, Sputnik 1 was launched by The Soviet Union, the first artificial Earth satellite to break into elliptical orbit. Ken Batcher had been working on an early version of a computer with Goodyear Aerospace at their Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant, producing uranium for the United States Atomic Energy Program. It was at this time Johnnie Baker remembered:
“the United States government thought they better put some money into math and science”
“and computers.” Added Dr. Batcher’s son, Dr. Kenneth Batcher jr.
This is where Dr. Batcher senior had his start in the world and development in computing.

Kenneth E. Batcher was born on December 27, 1935. Raised in the borough of Queens, New York City, he would never quite like the way of life in the city. Coming from a lineage of science and electronics, his father, Ralph R. Batcher was an early pioneer in the world of television, penning a column in the New York Sun for hobbyists to build their own versions of tube TVs. Graduating with a degree in Radio Engineering (Universities didn’t commonly offer electrical engineering degrees yet!) he spent both World Wars reverse engineering the technologies on the feared German U-Boat, in the intermittent and subsequent years he wrote his own notable work the “Electronic Engineering Handbook”. It was in this environment, where Ken Batcher’s was able to watch what is considered the first major new story to break on television: the attacks at Pearl Harbor. It was in this environment where Ken’s mind was fostered and able to flourish.

Early Life

Ken Batcher was born in December 1935 in Queens, New York City to Louis and Ralph Batcher.His parents met at Iowa State University and later relocated to New York City after graduation. His father, Ralph R. Batcher, was the Chief Engineer of The A. H. Grebe Radio Company until its bankruptcy in 1932.

Education

Batcher graduated from Iowa State University with B.E. degree in 1957. In 1964, Batcher received his Ph.D. in electrical engineering from the University of Illinois.

1953 High School Diploma - Brooklyn Technical High School, Brooklyn, NY.

1957 B.S.E.E. - Iowa State University, Ames, IA

1962 M.S. - University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign, IL

1964 Ph.D. - University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign, IL

Career

He worked for Goodyear Aerospace as a Computer Engineer and later as a professor at Kent State University (since 1989). 

Contributions

Dr. Kenneth E. Batcher was a figurehead in the field of Computer Science. He made numerous contributions and has left a lasting web of influence on those he spent his time with and with the community at large.

While working at Goodyear Aerospace and later at Kent State University, Dr. Batcher’s mainstay was the areas of parallel computation and associative computing. Associative computing is a term created by Dr. Jerry Potter who worked closely with Dr. Batcher, and the term essentially refers to everything in the realm of computational parallelization, the use of both data parallel programming and the ability to search memory by data content rather than memory address[1].

Some of the most impactful projects that Dr. Batcher was involved with were the development of two SIMD (single instruction, multiple data) parallel processors known as the STARAN and the Massively Parallel Processor (MPP), both of which were developed through Goodyear Aerospace. These works later inspired the Goodyear ASPRO system, which Dr. Batcher helped design initially before transitioning to Kent State University [2][3].

The STARAN was one of the first major computers designed around associative memory, and Dr. Batcher was its chief architect. It was built around 1972 [2] and its main application was in Air Traffic Control (ATC) systems. STARAN had multiple generations and was used by Goodyear as a basis for research for at least 30 years [7].

Following STARAN around 1983 [2], was the Goodyear Massively Parallel Processor (MPP). The MPP was originally built for NASA and was based around earlier technology developed for the STARAN [8]. The MPP was initially designed to quickly process satellite images, but it also found many other research and project applications through NASA [9]. Only one MPP was ever developed and is now held within the Smithsonian near the Space Shuttle Discovery [10].

Finally, the ASPRO was a second generation STARAN that was developed around the time Dr. Batcher transitioned to Kent State University in 1989. While he did not head the development of the ASPRO, he still worked closely with Goodyear in completing the system [11]. The ASPRO itself was built specifically for use by the U.S. Navy for surveillance, and it was even installed in Hawkeye Radar Planes in order to provide ATC at sea and track enemy targets. It is estimated that around 750 ASPROs were developed [12].

Dr. Batcher is also credited with discovering two parallel sorting algorithms: the odd-even mergesort and the bitonic mergesort [3]. Odd-even mergesort is an algorithm for sorting networks of size O(n (log n)2) and depth O((log n)2) [4]. Meanwhile, bitonic mergesort is essentially a parallel version [5] which the STARAN, MPP, and ASPRO all utilized in some variation [6].

Late Life

Dr. Kenneth Edward Batcher passed away in Stow, Ohio on August 22, 2019. Ken was 83 years old when he passed, and per his wishes, his body was donated to the Northeast Ohio Medical University to help further medical scientific studies. His obituary can be found on the Akron Beacon Journal website, where contributions can also be made in his memory to the Department of Anatomy and Neurobiology.

He is survived by his wife, Slyvia Batcher; his daughter, Joan Booth; his son, Kenneth W. Batcher; his grandchildren, Joshua, Lenora, Victoria, and Noah Batcher; and his brother, Noah Batcher.
